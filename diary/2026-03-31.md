## 2026-03-08 — Adaptive Top-K & Query Reformulation

**Notebook:** [`2026-03-08-adaptive-topk.ipynb`](../notebooks/2026-03-08-adaptive-topk.ipynb)

### Core finding: recall is the bottleneck, not the reranker cutoff

bge-m3 recall@80 is ~26% overall, collapsing to ~6% for queries with 10+ citations. Increasing the retrieval pool helps but the fundamental problem is semantic: queries describe *fact patterns* while gold articles describe *abstract legal norms*. These live in different embedding spaces.

### Adaptive cutoff strategies (Section 5)

Rather than a fixed top-10, explored data-driven cutoffs using the reranker score distribution:

| Strategy | Description |
|---|---|
| `fixed_k=10` | baseline |
| `fixed_k=20` | more candidates, lower precision |
| `largest_gap` | cut after the largest score drop in the ranked list |
| `gap_thresh=0.05/0.10/0.15` | cut where gap exceeds threshold |

Evaluated against median F1 on `train_clean`. Gap-threshold strategies beat fixed-k, but the gain was modest — the real problem is that missing gold articles are *never retrieved*, so no cutoff strategy can recover them.

**Section 4 finding:** largest-gap position has poor correlation with optimal cutoff. Score gap is a noisy signal for deciding where to stop.

### Query reformulation (Sections 7 / 7b)

**Section 7 — Naive reformulation:** use Qwen2.5 (3B locally, 7B on Kaggle) to rewrite the English fact-pattern query into German legal norm language before embedding.

Prompt instructs the model to output the abstract legal principle invoked, using Swiss statute vocabulary. Small-scale results showed improved recall on "missed" single-citation queries.

**Section 7b — Taxonomy-guided reformulation:** naive reformulation fails because the LLM stays in the surface domain (student exam → university regulations) instead of navigating to the correct legal branch (criminal law → obstruction of authorities). Fix: give the LLM an explicit 10-branch Swiss law taxonomy upfront. It first selects the applicable branch, then generates vocabulary anchored to that branch.

The taxonomy acts as a map — the LLM needs to know which room of the legal house to go to, not the exact article number. Qualitative inspection showed clear improvement in targeting correct law domains on the missed examples.

## 2026-03-13 — Law Text Characteristics Analysis

**Notebook:** [`2026-03-13-law-text-analysis.ipynb`](../notebooks/2026-03-13-law-text-analysis.ipynb)

Goal: diagnose why BERTopic produced ~42% outliers in the previous session.

### Key findings

**Section 3 — Cross-article cosine similarity:**
- Within-domain cosine mean ≈ cross-domain cosine mean → almost no natural cluster structure in embedding space
- The embedding model collapses legal domain differences into a narrow band of similarity
- Explains HDBSCAN outliers: no clear density peaks, everything is uniformly dense

**Section 4 — Domain breakdown:**
- Domain sizes vary wildly (some SR domains have hundreds of articles, others have thousands)
- Within-domain articles are only marginally more similar to each other than cross-domain pairs — cosine gap ≈ 0.0208 (baseline)

**Section 5 — TF-IDF per domain:**
- High-TF-IDF terms per domain are genuinely distinctive (e.g. tax law: `Steuer`, `Abzug`, `Einkommen`; criminal law: `Strafe`, `Täter`, `Vergehen`)
- But shared legalese (`Absatz`, `gemäss`, `Bundesrat`, `Artikel`) dominates raw frequency

**Section 7 — Vocabulary overlap:**
- Jaccard overlap between top-1000 words per domain is high — most domains share 40–60% of vocabulary
- TF-IDF is needed to find what's discriminative; raw frequency is too noisy

**Section 8 — TF-IDF embedding baseline:**
- Built TF-IDF vectors restricted to discriminative vocabulary per domain
- Compared against neural (untuned multilingual-e5, cosine gap ≈ 0.0208)
- TF-IDF baseline establishes a vocabulary-only floor; neural should beat it after fine-tuning
- kNN accuracy on gold test set gives a concrete accuracy metric to track

**Section 9 — Unknown article classification:**
- Applied the trained classifier (kNN on neural embeddings) to the 69k "unknown" domain articles
- Saved `unknown_classified.csv` with predicted domain + confidence per article

## 2026-03-14 — Domain Contrastive Fine-tuning (multilingual-e5-base)

**Notebook:** [`2026-03-14-domain-finetuning.ipynb`](../notebooks/2026-03-14-domain-finetuning.ipynb)

### Goal

Fine-tune `intfloat/multilingual-e5-base` with contrastive loss so that same-domain articles cluster tightly in embedding space. This is a prerequisite for domain-guided retrieval routing.

### Method

- **Loss:** `MultipleNegativesRankingLoss` — each positive pair `(anchor, positive)` uses all other in-batch articles as implicit negatives. No explicit negative mining needed.
- **Training data:** ~27k positive pairs, balanced across 9 SR domains, sampled from 101k labeled articles
- **E5 input format:** `passage: <title>. <text>` prefix is required (E5 models are trained with query:/passage: prefixes)
- **Evaluation:** within-domain vs cross-domain cosine similarity gap + 5-NN classification accuracy on gold test set

### Result

**Within/cross-domain cosine gap: 0.02 (untuned) → 0.62 (fine-tuned)**

This is a massive structural change — the fine-tuned model separates domains clearly. Domain-guided retrieval routing (retrieve within predicted domain first) becomes viable.

### Post-training

Saved fine-tuned model to `models/e5-domain-finetuned/` and used kNN (trained on labeled articles) to classify all 69k unknown articles → `unknown_classified.csv`.

## 2026-03-15 — Knowledge Graph Construction

**Notebook:** [`2026-03-15-build-kg.ipynb`](../notebooks/2026-03-15-build-kg.ipynb)  
**Script:** [`scripts/build_kg.py`](../scripts/build_kg.py) | **Package:** [`src/legal_kg/`](../src/legal_kg/)

### Approach

Run `google/gemma-2-9b-it` (4-bit NF4 quantization) on Kaggle to extract entity triples from Swiss law articles. Each article → structured `(subject, relation, object)` triples.

### Pipeline components

- **Extractor** (`src/legal_kg/extractor.py`): LLM-based triple extraction
- **Judge** (`src/legal_kg/judge.py`): LLM-as-critic to filter low-quality triples
- **Linker** (`src/legal_kg/linker.py`): seeds known Swiss-law abbreviations (BV, OR, ZGB, StGB, …) and resolves entity aliases
- **Store** (`src/legal_kg/store.py`): `networkx` graph, saves `entities.json` + `triples.jsonl`

First 500 sequential SR articles + 500 random articles processed. KG stats saved to `/kaggle/working/kg/`.

**Why:** KG could enable graph-based retrieval augmentation — start from query entities, hop through relations to relevant articles — as an alternative to pure dense retrieval for complex multi-hop legal questions.

## 2026-03-17 — Semantic Legal Domain Taxonomy (Gemma, 30 domains)

**Notebook:** [`2026-03-17-sr-taxonomy.ipynb`](../notebooks/2026-03-17-sr-taxonomy.ipynb)

### Problem with SR-number taxonomy

BGE court decisions have no SR numbers. A domain taxonomy derived from SR structure can't classify BGE cases. We need a unified vocabulary that covers both corpora.

### Approach

Define 30 semantic domain names (e.g. `Verfassungsrecht`, `Vertragsrecht`, `Strafrecht`, `Umweltrecht`, …) based on Swiss legal content, not administrative numbering. Then use Gemma-2-9b-it (4-bit) to classify:

1. **Each unique law** → reads title + Art. 1 text → assigns one of 30 domains
2. **Each BGE court case** → reads first consideration paragraph → assigns one of 30 domains

Both corpora end up with the same domain vocabulary → a single query classifier can narrow search across both.

### Output

- `law_domain_mapping.csv` — `law_id → domain`
- `court_domain_mapping.csv` — `case_id → domain`
- Section 6 validates against gold citations (query domain vs gold article domain agreement rate)

### Why this matters

Domain routing could let us do `retrieve within predicted_domain` instead of exhaustive search across all 170k+ articles — significant speedup and potential precision gain if query→domain classification is accurate.
