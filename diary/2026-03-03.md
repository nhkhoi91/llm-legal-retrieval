## What we found

**The cross-lingual problem is severe**
- Train queries are in German. Val and test queries are in English.
- The corpus is in German (plus French and Italian for court decisions).
- BM25 on an English query against a German corpus retrieves noise — English institution names, not law articles. The BM25 component in the baseline is actively hurting val/test scores.

**Fixed top-k is structurally broken**
- Train: median 2 citations per query, max 44.
- Val: median 22 citations per query.
- The baseline caps at `top_k_final=10`. For val, that means the correct answers are structurally outside the candidate set for most queries.
- Macro F1 penalizes both ends: too few citations tanks recall, too many tanks precision. Need adaptive top-k.

**The baseline wastes most of bge-m3's capacity**
- bge-m3 supports 8,192 tokens. Corpus texts are cut at 2,000 chars before embedding — using less than 10% of available capacity.
- 22% of train queries exceed 2,000 chars and get silently truncated. These are the complex Sachverhalt cases that need the most context.

**Court corpus: noisy and costly**
- 17.5% of court rows are under 100 chars — procedural one-liners with zero retrieval value.
- Only 1.2% of gold citations are court decisions.
- Talha found that adding the full court corpus *dropped* his score (laws-only: 0.01454 → laws+court: 0.01384). The noise cost exceeds the recall gain.
- The reranker truncates at 512 tokens. Court texts are long and have no newlines, so chunking them cleanly is non-trivial.

**Coverage: 28.8% missing from train, 0% from val**
- 28.8% of train gold citations are absent from the corpus (law codes OR, ZGB, StGB missing from `laws_de.csv`).
- Val: 0% missing. This is deliberate — organizers curated val/test to only reference present laws.
- Important caveat: some "missing" citations may be granularity mismatches — gold labels use `Art. X Abs. Y lit. Z` but corpus stores at `Art. X Abs. Y` level. Exact string match marks them missing even though the content is there. To investigate.

**Separate indices matter**
- Laws are short (median ~200 chars). Court are long (median ~936 chars).
- In a shared embedding space, long documents dominate similarity scores and bury short law articles.
- Solution: separate FAISS indices for laws vs court, search independently, combine results.

## Roadmap going forward

| Priority | Action | Rationale |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| High | Laws-only retrieval, no BM25, translate queries | BM25 broken cross-lingually; court adds noise |
| High | Adaptive top-k via score gap | Fixed top-k structurally fails on both ends |
| High | Raise char cutoff (2000 → full token limit) | 22% of queries truncated; bge-m3 has 8192 tok |
| Medium | Regex citation extraction from query | Free precision — extract explicit `Art. X StGB` mentions |
| Medium | Check citation granularity mismatch | 28.8% missing may be inflated by `lit. X` suffix |
| Medium | Separate FAISS indices (laws vs court) | Prevents length bias burying short laws |
| Low | Fine-tune embedder with hard negatives | Talha got +22% — worth doing after baseline is stable |
